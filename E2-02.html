Exam 2 - 02 - Probability Theory

1. Overall Learnings / Summary of the Topic

From Finite Probability
Experiment
A procedure that yields one of a given set of possible outcomes.

Sample Space
The set of possible outcomes

Event
A subset of the sample space. An event is part of all the possible outcomes.
p(E) = | E | / | S |
Where E is the number of outcomes divided by the total number of outcomes S.

Rules

 Let S be the sample space of an experiment with a finite or countable number of outcomes. We assign a probability p(s) to each outcome s. Two conditions must be met:
1. The probability of each outcome is a nonnegative real number no greater than 1.
2. The sum of the probabilities of all possible outcomes should be 1.

Definition 1

 Suppose that S is a set with n elements. The uniform distribution assigns the probability 1/n to each element of S.

Definition 2

 The probability of the event E is the sum of the probabilities of the outcomes in E.

2. Analysis

Probability theory is an extension of the analysis from finite probability. Probability theory describes in detail the rules and definitions of finite probability. It suggests that no probability is greater than 1 or less than 0 (negative probability). The sum of all likelihood of some event will happen is and should always be 1. One cannot say that there is a 1000% percent that something will happen in an event. Likewise, one cannot say that something will not happen by a -200%, as if the opposite of that event instead will happen. This topic sets the rules and boundaries of what a probability should be and how likely such a probability will happen.

Games and Entertainment

Example 2.2.1 - Die : Suppose that a die is biased so that 3 appears twice as often as each other number but that the other five outcomes are equally likely. What is the probability that an odd number appears when we roll this die?

Solution:
The probability of rolling a dice number other than 3 is
P(n) = x for n is an element of {1,2,3,4,5,6} - {3}
in which the dice numbers other than 3 are equally likely to appear.

P(3) = 2x since 3 appears twice as likely to appear than any other number. Thus by condition 2:
P(1) + P(2) + P(3) + P(4) + P(5) +P(6) = 1
x + x + 2x + x + x+ x = 1
7x = 1
x = 1/7

That means, p(1) = p(2) = p(4) = p(5) = p(6) = 1/7
p(3) = 2/7

Therefore p(E) = p(1) + p(3) + p(5) = 1/7 + 2/7 +1/7 = 4/7

Example 2.2.2 - Coin Toss: What probabilities should we assign to the outcomes H (heads) and T (tails) when a fair coin is flipped? What probabilities should be assigned to these outcomes when the coin is biased so that heads comes up twice as often as tails?

Solution:
Taken from the assumption that heads will come up twice as often as tails
p(H) = 2p(T)
From the second condition that all probabilities of a sample space are equal to 1
p(H) + p(T) = 1

Substitute:
2p(T) + p(T) = 1
3p(T) = 1
p(T) 1/3
We can conclude that p(T) = 1/3 and p(H) = 2/3. This means that the tails' chances of appearing are 1/3 while for the heads chances are 2/3.
